Agent Evaluation

Why Enterprise AI Agents Still
Fail at Real Work

Frontier models from every major lab solve fewer than 35 percent of workplace tasks when measured against expert-authored rubric criteria. The gap between benchmark scores and production readiness is larger than the industry acknowledges.

Arabic.AI Editorial
April 2026
12 min read
Based on: EnterpriseBench CoreCraft (Mehta et al., 2026)

In mid-2025, Surge AI built a simulated enterprise called CoreCraft. It was not a toy. The environment modeled a customer support organization for a PC hardware retailer with over 2,500 entities, 14 entity types, and 23 distinct tools. Agents inside CoreCraft had to navigate order databases, customer records, shipping manifests, return policies, and internal knowledge bases to complete multi-step support tasks. It was, in structure and complexity, closer to a real enterprise environment than anything the AI field had previously used as a benchmark.

Then Surge AI ran the best available AI models against it. The results were sobering. Frontier models including GPT-5.2, Claude Opus 4.6, and Gemini 3.1 Pro collectively solved fewer than 35 percent of tasks when all expert-authored rubric criteria were required to be satisfied. The strongest single model, GPT-5.2, achieved just 29.20 percent.

For organizations evaluating AI for deployment in regulated workflows, government services, or any environment where incomplete task execution has real consequences, these numbers carry significant weight. This article explains what the CoreCraft study reveals, why benchmark scores systematically misrepresent production readiness, and what a more rigorous evaluation standard looks like.

The Benchmark Problem

The AI industry has a measurement problem, and it has been accumulating for years. Standard benchmarks measure isolated capabilities: reading comprehension, mathematical reasoning, code generation, factual recall. These tests are useful for tracking model development, but they are structurally unable to answer the question that enterprise buyers actually need answered: can this model complete the work that my organization requires, under the conditions in which that work is performed?

The difference between a benchmark task and an enterprise task is not a matter of difficulty. It is a matter of structure. Benchmark tasks are self-contained, clearly bounded, and evaluated against a predetermined correct answer or a small set of correct answers. Enterprise tasks are open-ended, stateful, and evaluated against outcomes that depend on context, policy, and the specific history of interactions within a session.

A customer support agent that needs to determine whether a return is eligible must retrieve the original order date, verify it against the return policy, check whether the item was opened, apply any exceptions for defective goods, and communicate all of this accurately to the customer. Each of those steps requires a correct sub-decision. Getting four out of five right and failing the fifth does not produce a useful outcome. In a real support environment, partial completion can mean a refund processed incorrectly, a customer retained when they should have been escalated, or a policy exception granted without authorization.

"The gap between what models score on benchmarks and what they actually accomplish in realistic workflows is not a calibration problem. It is a measurement problem. We have been measuring the wrong things."

Analysis of EnterpriseBench CoreCraft findings, Surge AI 2026

CoreCraft was designed specifically to expose this gap. Every task in the environment was written by domain experts who also wrote the evaluation rubrics. A task was counted as passed only when every criterion in the rubric was satisfied. This is a stricter standard than most production systems apply, which is part of the point: regulated industries often require exactly this level of completeness, because incomplete task execution has legal, financial, or safety implications.

Three Failure Patterns That Persist Even in the Strongest Models

The CoreCraft study did not find that models failed randomly. The failures were structured and predictable. Three patterns appeared consistently across all evaluated models, including those that performed best overall.

Poor Search Strategy

When agents needed to locate specific information, they defaulted to generic search queries rather than constructing targeted ones. An agent asked about a customer order from a specific date would query "orders" rather than the order ID or the customer account. This produced results that were either too broad to act on or that required additional filtering steps the agent then failed to perform.

This failure is not a knowledge failure. The models have the information they need to construct precise queries. The failure is architectural: models trained on single-turn instruction following do not develop robust strategies for multi-step information retrieval, because that skill is rarely evaluated directly.

Failure to Paginate Through Incomplete Results

When database queries or tool calls returned partial results with pagination markers, models frequently treated the first page as complete. They did not recognize cues indicating that additional results were available, and they proceeded to answer based on an incomplete data set.

In practice, this means an agent might correctly determine that a customer is eligible for a return based on the most recent order visible in the results, while a second order from the same customer, which is outside the return window, goes unexamined. The agent gives a technically accurate answer about what it saw, but an operationally incorrect answer about the customer's actual situation.

Incomplete Tool Exploration

Agents consistently failed to use the full range of tools available to them. In environments with 23 distinct tools, models tended to rely on a subset of familiar tool types, such as simple database lookups and communication interfaces, while underusing diagnostic utilities, audit tools, and tools that required multi-step invocation sequences.

This is consistent with what you would expect from models trained primarily on documentation and code rather than on sequential, goal-directed tool use in stateful environments. The model knows that a tool exists. It does not have a robust policy for when to use it.

What Training on Realistic Environments Showed

The most technically significant finding in the CoreCraft study was not the baseline failure rates. It was what happened when a model was trained directly on the environment.

Surge AI took GLM 4.6, a 357-billion-parameter mixture-of-experts model with 32 billion active parameters, and fine-tuned it using Group Relative Policy Optimization with adaptive clipping, using CoreCraft's expert-authored rubrics as the reward signal. After a single epoch of training on 1,000 CoreCraft tasks, the model improved from a 25.37 percent to a 36.76 percent task pass rate on held-out evaluation tasks. That is an 11.39 percentage point improvement, which is larger than the capability gap between Claude Sonnet 4.5 and Claude Opus 4.5 on the same evaluation.

More significant than the in-distribution improvement was the transfer to benchmarks the model had never seen. Performance on BFCL Parallel function calling improved by 4.5 percent. On Tau2-Bench Retail, improvement was 7.4 percent. On Tool Decathlon at Pass@1, improvement was 6.8 percent. These benchmarks test capabilities that are structurally similar to CoreCraft tasks: multi-step retrieval, constraint satisfaction, and structured output generation.

The interpretation the researchers offer is that high-fidelity environment training teaches generalizable workflow patterns rather than domain-specific heuristics. The model did not learn how to work at a PC hardware retailer. It learned how to navigate multi-step, constraint-bound workflows with incomplete information, which is a transferable skill that applies across enterprise contexts.

Implications for Regulated Industries

For organizations in government, financial services, healthcare, or any sector where task completeness has compliance implications, the CoreCraft findings point toward several practical conclusions.

First, benchmark performance is not a reliable proxy for production readiness. A model that achieves 80 percent on a standard agentic benchmark may achieve 30 percent on tasks drawn from your actual operational workflows. The evaluation methodology determines the measurement, and standard methodologies are not designed for enterprise conditions.

Second, partial task completion may be more dangerous than no task completion in certain contexts. An agent that retrieves 80 percent of the relevant information and then gives a confident, partially correct answer creates a different risk profile than an agent that declines to act because it cannot access sufficient data. The former produces errors that look like correct outputs. The latter produces refusals that are easy to identify and route to a human.

Third, training methodology matters as much as base model capability. The CoreCraft results show that a model with lower baseline capability can outperform a stronger model after targeted training on high-fidelity enterprise tasks. This means that the choice of training environment is a consequential design decision for any organization building AI systems for operational use.

What This Means for Arabic Enterprise AI

The CoreCraft study was conducted in English, on a customer support scenario drawn from a fictional US-based PC hardware company. Its findings are structurally generalizable, but its specific data does not reflect the operational requirements of Arabic-language government systems, financial institutions in Gulf markets, or public services across the Arab world.

This distinction matters for two reasons. The first is linguistic. Arabic presents evaluation challenges that English-language benchmarks do not capture: morphological complexity, dialectal variation across 22 countries, formal versus colloquial register differences, and the distinct requirements of right-to-left text processing in structured data environments. A model evaluated on CoreCraft-style enterprise tasks in Modern Standard Arabic would face a materially harder evaluation than one evaluated in English.

The second is operational. Government and enterprise workflows in Arabic-speaking markets have specific policy structures, regulatory requirements, and organizational patterns that differ from the US enterprise model that CoreCraft simulates. An evaluation environment built for Arabic enterprise AI would need to reflect those structures to produce findings that are actionable for organizations in those markets.

The methodology demonstrated by CoreCraft, which is to say high-fidelity environment construction, expert-authored rubrics, and reinforcement learning on realistic workflows, is the right approach. The specific implementation requires localization to be valid for Arabic enterprise contexts. That is a substantial technical undertaking, and it is one that the Arabic AI community has not yet systematically addressed.

Conclusion

The CoreCraft study is one of the clearest demonstrations to date of the distance between AI agent capability as measured by standard benchmarks and AI agent capability as required by realistic enterprise deployment. The 35 percent ceiling observed across frontier models is not a ceiling set by the fundamental limits of current model architectures. It is a ceiling set by how models are trained and evaluated, and it is a ceiling that can be raised through targeted training on high-fidelity environments with expert-authored reward signals.

For enterprise and government buyers, the practical implication is this: demand evaluation against tasks drawn from your actual operational environment, with rubrics authored by domain experts who understand what task completion actually requires. Accept benchmark scores as a starting point for comparison, not as evidence of production readiness. And treat partial task completion not as near-success but as a distinct failure mode with its own risk profile.

The AI field is capable of building agents that can do real work. The evidence also shows that the field has not yet delivered them at the reliability levels that regulated industries require. Closing that gap requires better environments, better rubrics, and a more honest accounting of what evaluation actually measures.
