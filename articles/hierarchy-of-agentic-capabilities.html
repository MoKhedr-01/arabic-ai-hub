Agentic Capabilities

The Hierarchy of Agentic Capabilities:
What Frontier Models Still Cannot Do

An empirical study across 150 workplace tasks in a realistic e-commerce environment reveals a clear ordering of capabilities that AI agents must master before they are deployable in complex operational settings. Even the best-performing frontier models fail approximately 40 percent of tasks.

Arabic.AI Editorial
April 2026
10 min read
Based on: Ritchie et al. (2026), Surge AI

When an AI agent fails at a workplace task, the failure is rarely random. Across 150 workplace tasks evaluated against a realistic e-commerce reinforcement learning environment, Ritchie et al. (2026) found that agent failures cluster predictably along a defined hierarchy of capabilities. Weaker models fail at the lower levels of the hierarchy. Stronger models fail higher up. But every model tested, including the frontier models, fails at something.

The hierarchy the study identifies has five levels: tool use, planning and goal formation, adaptability, groundedness, and common-sense reasoning. These are not arbitrary categories. They are empirically derived from the patterns of success and failure observed across models and tasks, and they describe something real about the structure of agentic capability. Understanding the hierarchy is useful for any organization evaluating AI agents for deployment, because it provides a principled framework for predicting where a given model will fail and what that failure will look like.

The Five Levels of the Hierarchy

The hierarchy is ordered from foundational to advanced. An agent that fails at a lower level will also fail at higher levels. An agent that succeeds at a higher level has demonstrated competence at all levels below it.

Level 1: Tool Use

The most fundamental capability is the ability to invoke the correct tool for a given task and to invoke it correctly. This sounds simple, but tool use in realistic environments requires selecting among many available tools, understanding which tool is appropriate for which information need, constructing valid API calls with the correct parameters, and handling tool failures or empty results without derailing the overall task.

Weaker models fail at this level consistently. They invoke tools that are not appropriate for the task, construct malformed queries, and do not recover from tool failures. Stronger models pass this level but show characteristic failure patterns in how they use tools: they may invoke the right tool but with overly generic parameters, or fail to recognize that a tool result is incomplete and requires follow-up.

Level 2: Planning and Goal Formation

The second level requires the agent to decompose a complex goal into a sequence of sub-goals, to maintain that decomposition across multiple tool calls and intermediate results, and to update the plan when intermediate results reveal that the original plan was based on incorrect assumptions.

Models that cannot plan at this level tend to approach tasks linearly, executing a fixed sequence of steps without considering whether the steps are appropriate given what they have learned from intermediate results. They complete the steps rather than the task.

Level 3: Adaptability

The third level requires the agent to recognize when circumstances require a departure from the planned approach. A customer who changes their stated preference mid-conversation, a policy exception that applies to a specific case, a tool result that reveals a constraint the agent was not aware of: adaptability is the capacity to recognize these situations and adjust accordingly.

This is where many models that appear capable in simple evaluations reveal their limits. Adaptation requires recognizing that the current plan is no longer correct, which requires a form of self-monitoring that models are not consistently trained to do.

Level 4: Groundedness

Groundedness is the capacity to constrain outputs to information that is actually supported by the available evidence. An agent that is not grounded will fill gaps in its information with plausible-sounding content that is not based on the actual data it has accessed. In enterprise contexts, this produces outputs that appear complete and confident but contain fabricated details.

Level 5: Common-Sense Reasoning

The highest level of the hierarchy is common-sense reasoning: the capacity to make inferences that go beyond explicit instructions when the task requires them. A customer who has placed three orders in the last month and is now requesting a return on each of them is exhibiting a pattern that a human agent would recognize as potentially problematic without being explicitly told to watch for it. Common-sense reasoning is what enables this kind of contextual inference.

Even the strongest frontier models fail at this level on a significant fraction of tasks. The failures are not random: they cluster around tasks where the relevant inference requires combining information from multiple sources in ways that were not specified in the task instructions.

How Failures Distribute Across the Hierarchy

The study's most useful practical finding is the failure distribution. Weaker models fail predominantly at levels 1 and 2, with fundamental tool use and planning errors accounting for the majority of failures. Stronger models clear these levels reliably but accumulate failures at levels 3 through 5, with common-sense reasoning being the level where the strongest models show the highest concentration of remaining failures.

Stronger models primarily fail on tasks requiring contextual inference beyond explicit instructions. This is a different failure mode from the tool use and planning failures that dominate weaker models, and it requires a different approach to address.

Analysis of Ritchie et al. (2026)

This distribution is informative for procurement decisions. If your operational tasks require frequent contextual inference that goes beyond explicit instructions, the capability gap between a strong model and a weaker model at levels 1 through 3 is less relevant than their relative performance at level 5. And if even the strongest models fail 40 percent of tasks that require level-5 reasoning, that is a direct input to the question of whether AI agents are ready for autonomous operation in that context or whether human oversight is required.

Implications for Regulated Deployment

For organizations in regulated industries, the hierarchy provides a structured way to think about risk. Failures at lower levels of the hierarchy tend to be more visible: the agent invokes the wrong tool and gets an error, or it produces an output that is clearly not responsive to the task. These failures are easier to detect and route to human review.

Failures at higher levels are more dangerous precisely because they are less visible. An agent that fails at groundedness produces an output that looks complete and confident but contains information it invented. An agent that fails at common-sense reasoning produces an output that is locally correct but misses a contextual implication that a human would have caught. These failures may not be apparent from the output alone, and they may pass automated review without triggering an alert.

The practical implication is that monitoring and oversight systems for AI agents should be calibrated to the hierarchy. Detection logic that is designed to catch tool use failures will not catch groundedness failures. Organizations that deploy AI agents in regulated contexts need oversight mechanisms that are specifically designed to detect the failure modes that correspond to the capability levels their deployed models have not reliably mastered.

The Arabic Enterprise Context

The hierarchy described by Ritchie et al. is derived from English-language task evaluation in an English-language enterprise environment. The five levels of the hierarchy are structurally general, but the specific failure patterns at each level will differ for Arabic-language agents operating in Arabic enterprise contexts.

Arabic-language tool use, for example, introduces challenges that English-language evaluation does not encounter: queries constructed in Arabic may interact differently with database systems designed primarily for Latin-script input, and right-to-left text handling in structured data contexts creates failure modes that are not present in English agentic evaluation.

Similarly, common-sense reasoning at level 5 is heavily dependent on cultural context. The contextual inferences that a human agent in an Arabic-speaking enterprise context would make are not identical to those that an agent operating in a US enterprise context would make. Models trained predominantly on English-language data may have particularly large gaps at level 5 when deployed in Arabic enterprise contexts, because the cultural knowledge that underpins common-sense reasoning is not equally represented in their training data.

Conclusion

The hierarchy of agentic capabilities provides a more precise vocabulary for evaluating and discussing AI agent readiness than the binary of capable versus not capable. It allows organizations to identify specifically which capabilities a model has reliably demonstrated, which it demonstrates inconsistently, and which it consistently fails to exhibit. That specificity is necessary for making informed decisions about where AI agents can be deployed autonomously and where human oversight is required.

The finding that even frontier models fail approximately 40 percent of complex workplace tasks is not an argument against deploying AI agents. It is an argument for deploying them with clarity about which failure modes to expect, how to detect them, and what the appropriate human response is when they occur.
