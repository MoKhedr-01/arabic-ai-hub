LLM Evaluation

How Human Preference Data
Should Actually Be Collected

Standard human preference evaluation is demographically unrepresentative, reductive in its measurement, and poorly aligned with how people actually experience AI systems. The HUMAINE framework, built on 23,404 participants across 22 demographic groups, proposes a more rigorous approach.

Arabic.AI Editorial
April 2026
11 min read
Based on: Petrova, Gordon, Blindow (2026), arXiv:2603.04409

The dominant method for evaluating large language models against human judgment is the pairwise preference ranking. A human annotator sees two model outputs and selects the one they prefer. Aggregate those selections across many annotators and many prompts, apply a ranking model, and you have a preference leaderboard. Platforms like Chatbot Arena have made this approach widely accessible, and it has become a primary reference point for comparing frontier models.

The HUMAINE study, conducted by Petrova, Gordon, and Blindow (2026), is the most rigorous examination to date of what this methodology actually measures, and what it misses. Across 23,404 participants stratified by 22 demographic groups in the US and UK, evaluating 28 models across five distinct human-centric dimensions, the study identifies three systematic problems with standard preference evaluation and proposes a framework designed to address them.

28 State-of-the-art models evaluated across five dimensions
5 Human-centric evaluation dimensions beyond single preference ranking

Three Structural Problems with Standard Preference Evaluation

The HUMAINE study is precise about what it is critiquing. The problems it identifies are not failures of execution on individual platforms. They are structural features of the preference evaluation methodology as it is currently practiced, and they apply broadly.

Unrepresentative Sampling

Standard preference evaluation relies on annotator pools that are not demographically representative of the populations that use AI systems. Annotators tend to be younger, more technically literate, more concentrated in urban areas, and less diverse in linguistic background than the user populations whose preferences are supposedly being measured. The practical effect is that models are optimized for and evaluated against a segment of users rather than a representative sample.

This matters because the HUMAINE data shows that demographic group has a measurable effect on model preference rankings. Different groups rate models differently on different dimensions. A model that scores highest among technically literate urban users may not score highest among government workers in rural areas, or among users whose primary language is not English.

Single-Metric Reductionism

Pairwise preference ranking collapses a complex multi-dimensional judgment into a single binary output. The annotator prefers A to B, or B to A. This provides no information about why the preference was formed, which dimensions drove it, or whether the preference would hold in a different context or task.

The HUMAINE framework measures preferences across five separate dimensions: task complexity, goal achievement, communication quality, trustworthiness, and user experience. These dimensions do not move together. A model that ranks first on goal achievement may rank third on communication quality, and the aggregated single ranking conceals that distinction.

Superficial Assessment Depth

Standard preference annotation is typically applied to single-turn or very short multi-turn interactions. Real AI usage involves longer conversations with evolving context, sessions where the user's goal shifts, and interactions where trust is built or broken across multiple exchanges. Evaluations that sample short interactions miss the dynamics that determine how users actually experience AI systems at scale.

Optimizing for benchmark performance alone risks developing models that are technically impressive but fail to meet actual human needs. The evaluation gap has real consequences for how AI systems perform in practice.

HUMAINE Framework study, Petrova et al. (2026)

The HUMAINE Approach

The HUMAINE framework addresses each of these problems with specific methodological choices. Participants were recruited through stratified sampling designed to match demographic distributions in the US and UK census data. Post-stratification weights were applied to ensure that the aggregate preference measurements reflect the broader population rather than the annotator pool.

The preference comparison model used in HUMAINE is a hierarchical Bayesian implementation of the Bradley-Terry-Davidson model, which allows for uncertainty quantification and the modelling of multi-level demographic effects. This provides not just a point estimate of which model is preferred, but a probability distribution over rankings, which is more informative for decision-making under uncertainty.

The five-dimension evaluation structure was developed through a process that combined quantitative measurement on 1-to-5 scales with qualitative analysis and categorical classification. An LLM judge was used not as a proxy for human preference, but as a tool for structured post-hoc analysis of conversations, to help explain the mechanisms behind the preference patterns the human annotators produced.

Key Findings and What They Imply

The study's headline finding is a clear model ranking, with Gemini 2.5 Pro achieving the highest overall score with a 95.6 percent posterior probability of being the top-ranked model. But the more important findings for AI development and procurement are the structural ones about how rankings vary by demographic group and evaluation dimension.

Different demographic groups show systematically different preference patterns. Age, education level, and primary language all have measurable effects on which models are preferred and why. This finding has direct implications for any organization deploying AI systems to a specific population: the aggregate leaderboard ranking may not be the right selection criterion if your user population differs significantly from the evaluation sample.

The five-dimension structure also reveals that models with similar aggregate rankings have different strength profiles. A procurement decision that specifies which dimensions matter most for a given use case should use dimension-specific rankings rather than aggregate rankings.

Why Arabic AI Evaluation Requires a Different Approach

The HUMAINE study was conducted with participants in the US and UK, evaluating English-language model outputs. Its methodology is directly applicable to Arabic AI evaluation, but the implementation requires substantial modification.

Arabic-speaking populations span 22 countries with significant variation in dialect, formal language register, literacy patterns, and relationship to digital technology. A demographically representative Arabic preference evaluation cannot use census data from two countries. It requires sampling frameworks that reflect the actual distribution of Arabic speakers across Gulf, Levantine, North African, and Egyptian market contexts, with appropriate weighting for the specific deployment context.

Beyond demographics, Arabic language evaluation introduces additional dimensions that are not present in English evaluation. Dialect appropriateness, formal versus colloquial register selection, right-to-left text handling in structured data contexts, and code-switching between Arabic and English or French are all dimensions on which Arabic-language models differ in ways that single-metric preference ranking cannot capture.

The HUMAINE methodology provides the right framework for this evaluation. The implementation requires building the infrastructure specific to Arabic-speaking populations and Arabic-language assessment dimensions. That infrastructure does not yet exist in a standardized form, and its absence means that Arabic AI evaluation currently relies on English-language benchmarks and small-sample annotation efforts that produce measurements with significant uncertainty.

Conclusion

The HUMAINE framework represents a more rigorous standard for human preference evaluation than the methods currently in widespread use. Its requirements, demographically stratified sampling, multi-dimensional measurement, and probabilistic ranking models, are technically demanding but not beyond the reach of organizations that treat evaluation as a first-class engineering problem.

For the Arabic AI community, the framework also defines a gap. There is no equivalent of HUMAINE for Arabic-language AI. Building it would require significant investment in participant recruitment, evaluation infrastructure, and dimension design specific to Arabic language use across the populations that Arabic AI systems are intended to serve. That investment is not currently being made at scale, and the absence of it means that model selection for Arabic enterprise AI is being made without the quality of measurement that informed selection requires.
